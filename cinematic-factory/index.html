01 · Overview

Brief in. Cinema out.

Most "AI ads" look like AI ads. The Cinematic Factory exists to do the opposite — to produce single 15–30 second hero spots that feel like they came out of a real production house, but cost a fraction and ship in days. This is the operating manual.

The core thesis.

Cinema is reproducible. Every viral cinematic ad — Apple's "Shot on iPhone," Liquid Death's "Plastic Demon," Nike's hero pieces, Old Spice's surreal sketches — is built from the same finite library of beats, lenses, pacing decisions, and emotional arcs. We've deconstructed that library into a structured variable schema. The Factory takes a brand brief, selects the right genre and structure based on product fit, fills the variables, and runs an 8-agent production pipeline end-to-end.

The promise

A DTC brand provides a brief. 72 hours later, they receive a single 15–30s cinematic hero spot that meets a measurable DR floor (CTR, hook rate, hold rate) and a viral ceiling (saves, shares, watch-through). Same system. Any brand. Any product category.

What this is not.

  • Not a UGC factory. UGC has its own pipeline. This is for hero, brand-defining, top-of-funnel cinematic.
  • Not a 30-variant test pack. One spot per brief. Optimized to be the best single ad they could run, not the most ads they could run.
  • Not generic AI footage stitched together. Every shot is composed, color-graded, and edited against a structured beat sheet.
  • Not a replacement for live action when live action is the right call. The system flags when a brief needs hybrid or live action and routes accordingly.

How it fits the Ad Pros stack.

The Factory plugs into the existing 8-step intelligence flow — Collect → Organize → Remember → Think → Plan → Assign → Review → Improve — the same backbone that powers Workflow Factory and the Always-On Intelligence Layer. Brand briefs land in Collect, get structured into the variable schema in Organize, are matched against the reference library in Remember, scripted and storyboarded in Think, scheduled across the production pipeline in Plan, executed by the 8 agents in Assign, gated through Eval in Review, and fed back into the library in Improve.

Operating tenets.

TENET 01

Cinema is structured.

Every cinematic ad is reducible to a schema: hook archetype, narrative structure, genre, beat sequence, lens grammar, color palette, sound design, payoff type. We treat the schema as the product.

TENET 02

DR floor is non-negotiable.

The spot has to make money. Hook rate, hold rate, CTR, and CAC are measured before any subjective judgment is applied. If it's beautiful but doesn't convert, it's killed.

TENET 03

Genre is diagnostic, not stylistic.

The Recommender agent picks genre based on category, audience, and product physics — not founder vibes. A supplement brand might map to Liquid Death; an oat milk to A24. Fit beats taste.

TENET 04

Every shot is load-bearing.

15–30 seconds is brutal. There are no transition shots. Every frame must do narrative, sensory, brand, or DR work — usually two of those at once. The beat sheet enforces this.

TENET 05

Library compounds.

Every spot we ship gets evaluated, tagged, and added to the reference library — winning patterns get weighted up, losers get weighted down. The Factory gets sharper with every brief.

TENET 06

Humans gate the three moments.

Brand approval after the brief, creative approval after the storyboard, final approval before deploy. Everything else is automated. Three gates, no more, no less.

03 · Genre Library

Six cinematic archetypes.

Genre is the visual and tonal grammar the Factory dresses the chosen narrative structure in. Each archetype carries its own lens choices, color palette, pacing rules, music genre, and shot library. The Recommender selects one primary and one secondary genre per brief — most spots blend two for differentiation.

Reference · Apple, Aesop, Loro Piana

Luxury Minimal

Slow, premium, deliberate. Negative space as the loudest design choice. Product as artifact.

85mm · 24fps · macro · cool whites · ambient pad · 8–12 cuts
Reference · Nike, Hoka, Lululemon hero

Athletic Hero

Kinetic, emotional, maximalist. Sweeping movement, voiceover-led, the customer as protagonist.

35mm · 60fps · handheld · warm contrast · cinematic score · 18–24 cuts
Reference · Liquid Death, Loop Earplugs, Magic Spoon

Disruptor Absurd

Irreverent, attention-hijacking, cinematically over-produced jokes. Treats DTC like indie film.

28mm · 24fps · saturated · synth · whip-pans · 14–20 cuts
Reference · A24 trailers, Equinox, Mubi

Atmospheric Mood

Moody, character-driven, slow-burn cinema. Often dialogue-light. Product as object in a world.

50mm · 24fps · low-key · grain · ambient drone · 6–10 cuts
Reference · Old Spice, Dr Squatch, Skittles

CGI Maximalist

Surreal, hyper-stylized, joke-as-set-piece. AI-generated worlds where physics negotiates.

wide · 30fps · hyper-saturated · funk · 20+ cuts
Reference · Patagonia, Allbirds, Athletic Greens founder

Documentary Founder

Authentic, talking-head + b-roll, founder as protagonist. Earned credibility as the hook.

35mm · 24fps · natural light · sync sound · 10–14 cuts

Genre blending rules.

Most winners aren't pure-genre. The Factory blends a primary (60–70% of the visual grammar) with a secondary (30–40%) to create differentiation. Some proven blends:

  • Luxury Minimal × Disruptor Absurd → premium product, irreverent voiceover. Magic Spoon, Olipop's best-performing units.
  • Athletic Hero × Documentary Founder → hero shots intercut with founder testimony. Standard for performance supplements, wearables.
  • Atmospheric Mood × Luxury Minimal → slow cinema for premium DTC. Skincare, candles, high-AOV apparel.
  • CGI Maximalist × Disruptor Absurd → highest viral ceiling. Almost always wins on hook rate; needs the sharpest CTA to stick the DR landing.
  • Documentary Founder × Athletic Hero → founder + customer transformation. Best for category-defining brands.

Reference library spec.

Seed library: 30 cinematic ads (5 per genre), each deconstructed into a structured pattern card with hook archetype, lens grammar, color palette, beat sequence, sound design, and outcome metrics where available. Living library: every spot the Factory ships is added with evaluated performance, weighting future selections toward winning patterns. Stored in BigQuery cinematic_library.pattern_cards, embedded into pgvector for semantic retrieval at the Shape stage.

04 · Cinematic Beats

The six non-negotiables.

Every spot the Factory ships includes all six beat types. Their order, weight, and duration shifts based on the chosen narrative structure and genre — but skipping one is forbidden. These are the load-bearing moments the entire cinematic experience rests on.

BEAT 01 · 0–3 SECONDS
Pattern Interrupt
An unexpected scene, object, or composition that breaks scrolling. Function: forces a stop. Not the hook — the prerequisite to the hook. Common forms: an impossible visual, a cold open at peak action, an extreme close-up of an unexpected texture, a celebrity or character in the wrong context.
BEAT 02 · 1–6 SECONDS (OVERLAPS)
Sensory Hijack
A close-up, slow-mo, or ASMR moment that feels through the screen. Function: bypass the rational brain. The pour, the bite, the fizz, the stretch, the texture, the sound design. This is what makes a 30-second spot feel like an experience, not a pitch.
BEAT 03 · 6–12 SECONDS
Character Emotional Beat
A face, a reaction, a moment of human payoff. Function: emotional anchoring — the audience needs someone to feel something through. Even in product-led spots, there is always a face. Even if it's the founder's. Even if it's two seconds.
BEAT 04 · 7–18 SECONDS
Transformation Moment
The before/after, the state change, the visual evidence the product does what it claims. Function: proves the promise without saying it. Can be physical (skin, energy), behavioral (ritual change), or world-level (the room shifts). Always shown, never told.
BEAT 05 · 18–25 SECONDS
Hero Product Shot
A clean, iconic frame of the product — packaging, label, or use-state, perfectly lit and composed. Function: burn the SKU into memory. The Apple shot. The Liquid Death can. The frame the audience could draw from memory. Without this, brand recall collapses.
BEAT 06 · 25–30 SECONDS
Brand World & CTA
A final frame that places the product in its universe — vibe, location, lifestyle — paired with the direct response stamp (URL, offer, scan). Function: tie the cinematic experience back to a measurable click. Not optional. Not subtle. The CTA earns its keep.

Why this matters

06 · Agent System

Eight agents. Three gates.

The Factory runs the same 8-agent architecture as Workflow Factory — Recommender, Intake, Shape, parallel Designers, Skills Curator, Eval, Deploy, Improve — adapted for cinematic production. Three human gates: brand approval, creative approval, final approval. Everything else is autonomous.

AGENT 01 · RECOMMENDER

Genre & structure selection.

Reads the brand brief, maps it against the audience awareness level, product format, and hero claim format. Selects narrative structure (1 of 3) and genre primary + optional secondary (1–2 of 6). Outputs a recommendation with reasoning the human gate can accept or override.

Model · Claude Opus 4.7 · Reads pattern_cards via pgvector
AGENT 02 · INTAKE

Brief → schema.

Takes any tier of input — minimal brief, mid-tier brief, full intake — and parses it into the canonical variable schema. Asks clarifying questions only when a required field is missing. Surfaces gaps to the human before they become problems downstream.

Model · Claude Opus 4.7 · Tool · ClickUp form, BigQuery write
AGENT 03 · SHAPE

Beat sheet & script.

Given structure + genre + variables, generates the 8-beat sheet with per-beat shot description, duration, lens, voiceover (if any), sound design intent, and on-screen text. Outputs a fully scoped script + beat sheet ready for storyboarding.

Model · Claude Opus 4.7 · Reads pattern_cards · Outputs JSON
AGENT 04 · STAGE DESIGNERS ×8

Per-beat generation.

Eight parallel agents, one per beat, each responsible for generating the visual asset for its beat. Each runs an image-gen → motion-gen pipeline (Midjourney/Flux → Veo 3/Sora/Runway), then QAs against the beat brief. Failures retry with adjusted prompts up to 3x.

Models · Image & video gen APIs · Parallel execution
AGENT 05 · SKILLS CURATOR

Asset & library management.

Maintains the prompt library, model versions, brand asset packs, and reference pattern cards. Ensures every Stage Designer pulls the most current brand guideline assets and the highest-weighted reference patterns for the chosen genre.

Storage · BigQuery + Supabase pgvector + R2
AGENT 06 · EVAL

DR & cinema scoring.

Scores the assembled spot on two axes: DR floor (predicted hook rate, hold rate, CTR clarity, CTA visibility) and cinema ceiling (composition, pacing, color coherence, sound design). Both must pass thresholds. If not, routes back to Shape with structured feedback.

Model · Claude Opus 4.7 · Multimodal scoring
AGENT 07 · DEPLOY

Edit, render, handoff.

Assembles the final cut in Remotion: timing, transitions, music sync, color grade, captions, brand stamp, CTA. Outputs platform-specific renders (9:16 TikTok/Reels, 1:1 Feed, 4:5 Story-safe) with proper safe zones. Pushes to Frame.io and ClickUp for final gate.

Tools · Remotion · ElevenLabs · Frame.io · ClickUp
AGENT 08 · IMPROVE

Performance → library.

After spot ships, ingests Meta/TikTok/CTV performance back into BigQuery. Tags the spot's pattern card with outcome data. Re-weights the reference library — winning patterns rise, losers sink. Future Recommender calls inherit the sharpened weighting.

Source · BigQuery · Output · Updated pgvector embeddings

Three human gates.

G1
Gate 01 · Brand Approval

After Intake + Recommender complete. Brand approves the variable schema, structure, and genre selection before any production runs. Median time: 24h.

G2
Gate 02 · Creative Approval

After Shape outputs script + beat sheet, before Stage Designers fire. Brand reviews the storyboard, voiceover script, beat-by-beat intent. Median time: 24h. Cheapest gate to fail at — re-shape is fast.

G3
Gate 03 · Final Approval

After Deploy. Brand reviews the assembled spot, signs off, and authorizes platform handoff. Most expensive gate to fail — re-renders cost compute. Aim for ≤10% rejection rate via stronger Gate 2 work.

07 · Prompt Stack

The system prompts.

The agents are only as good as their prompts. Below is the production prompt skeleton each agent uses. Variables in {{ yellow }} are populated from the schema. These are abridged; full versions live in the Skills Curator's pattern library.

Recommender prompt

// Selects structure + genre based on brand fit, not taste

You are the Recommender Agent for the Cinematic Ad Factory.

INPUT: A populated brand variable schema.

YOUR JOB:
1. Diagnose the brand's audience awareness level and product physics
2. Select ONE narrative structure: hook-tension-reveal | transformation | character-conflict
3. Select ONE primary genre and OPTIONALLY one secondary genre (30-40%)
4. Output reasoning the human can accept or override

DECISION RULES:
- Cold audiences + sharp single claim → hook-tension-reveal
- State-change products (drinks, supplements, skincare) → transformation
- Identity products + warm audience → character-conflict
- High AOV + premium category → luxury-minimal or atmospheric-mood
- Disruptor in commodity category → disruptor-absurd or cgi-maximalist
- Founder-led category-defining brand → documentary-founder

OUTPUT FORMAT: JSON matching system_decisions schema.

Brand: {{ brand.name }}
Category: {{ brand.category }}
Hero claim: {{ product.hero_claim }}
Audience awareness: {{ audience.awareness_level }}
Persona: {{ audience.persona }}
DR target CAC: {{ dr.target_cac }}

Shape prompt (script + beat sheet)

// Generates 8-beat sheet from structure + genre + schema

You are the Shape Agent. You produce the script and beat sheet.

You MUST produce all 6 cinematic beats:
1. Pattern Interrupt
2. Sensory Hijack
3. Character Emotional Beat
4. Transformation Moment
5. Hero Product Shot
6. Brand World & CTA

Their order, weight, and duration follow the chosen structure: {{ system.structure }}
Visual grammar follows the genre: {{ system.genre_primary }} (+ {{ system.genre_secondary }})

For EACH of 8 timeline slots, output:
- duration_sec: number
- beat_type: one of the 6 + "narrative connective"
- shot_description: 1 paragraph, visual specifics
- lens: focal length + framing
- voiceover: text or null
- on_screen_text: text or null
- sound_design: 1 sentence

Hard constraints:
- Total = {{ system.duration }} seconds, ±0.5s
- Hero Product Shot must occupy ≥3 seconds
- Brand & CTA must occupy ≥3 seconds
- No more than 25% of duration without product on screen
- Hook (first 3s) must include product OR pattern interrupt

Output: JSON, no preamble.

// Two-axis scoring. Both must pass to deploy.

You are the Eval Agent. You score the assembled spot on two axes.

DR FLOOR (must score ≥7/10):
- Hook clarity (0-3s tells the audience what this is about): __ /10
- Product visibility (product on screen ≥75% of duration): __ /10
- CTA clarity (last 3s has unambiguous next step): __ /10
- Claim retention (hero claim appears as VO or text): __ /10
- Stop probability on TikTok/Reels feed: __ /10

CINEMA CEILING (must score ≥7/10):
- Composition consistency across beats: __ /10
- Color coherence to brand palette: __ /10
- Pacing serves the narrative structure: __ /10
- Sound design supports visual beats: __ /10
- Memorability — would you recall this 24h later?: __ /10

If EITHER axis < 7/10, output structured feedback for the Shape Agent
to revise the beat sheet. Specify which beats failed and why.

If BOTH axes ≥ 7/10, output APPROVED + brief notes for Deploy.
Prompt versioning

Prompts live in the Skills Curator's pattern library, versioned by date and tagged by performance outcome. When a spot wins (low CAC, high hold rate), its full prompt chain is pinned. When a spot loses, its prompts are tagged for regression analysis. The Factory is literally learning from itself.

08 · Intake Tiers

Tiered input, tiered output.

The deeper the brand brief, the sharper the spot. Four intake tiers — from a 30-minute form to a full strategic intake — each with a defined output quality and turnaround. Brands self-select based on stakes, budget, and timeline.

Tier 01 · Spark
30-minute form.
Brand name, product, one hero claim, three references. The Intake agent infers everything else.
  • Turnaround: 48h
  • Output: 1 cinematic spot, 15s
  • Genre lock: System chooses
  • Eval threshold: 6.5/10 both axes
  • Best for: Test-and-learn brands, new spend
Tier 02 · Standard
90-min intake.
Full schema fields A through D. Brand provides 5 reference ads they love and 2 they hate.
  • Turnaround: 72h
  • Output: 1 cinematic spot, 15 or 30s
  • Genre lock: Brand can override
  • Eval threshold: 7/10 both axes
  • Best for: Established DTC, monthly hero refresh
Tier 03 · Premium
Strategic intake.
Full A–D schema + audience research + competitive deconstruction + custom reference library build.
  • Turnaround: 7 days
  • Output: 1 hero spot, 30s + cutdowns
  • Genre lock: Co-decided in workshop
  • Eval threshold: 8/10 both axes
  • Best for: Campaign launches, new SKUs, brand refresh
Tier 04 · Bespoke
Hybrid live action.
Full Tier 3 + AI flagged that hybrid is required. Live action b-roll directed by Ad Pros, cut into the AI cinematic backbone.
  • Turnaround: 14–21 days
  • Output: 1 hero spot, 30s, broadcast-ready
  • Genre lock: Co-directed
  • Eval threshold: 9/10 both axes
  • Best for: Brand campaigns, CTV, broadcast

When does the system require hybrid live action?

  • Real product physics that current gen video can't fake — beverages with carbonation, complex liquid dynamics, foaming, specific clinical hardware.
  • Real founder presence required — documentary-founder genre with high-trust audience demands a real face, not an avatar.
  • Talent licensing — when a celebrity, athlete, or known creator is the hook.
  • Regulatory environment — supplements/health tech where claims must be presented by a real human in a verifiable context.
  • Brand maturity — when the brand has cultivated visual integrity that AI generation can't yet match.
09 · Quality & DR

How we know it works.

Beauty isn't a metric. The Factory ships against a defined DR floor and a defined cinema ceiling — both quantified, both gated. Below is the scoring rubric Eval applies before any spot leaves the system, and the post-flight measurement that feeds the library back.

Cinema ceiling (differentiator).

CriterionDefinitionThreshold

Genre fidelityVisual grammar matches selected genre archetype≥8/10

Post-flight measurement.

Once a spot ships, the Improve agent ingests platform data into BigQuery — Meta, TikTok, CTV — and tags the spot's pattern card with outcomes. Four metrics matter:

  • Hook rate (3s view / impression) — telegraphs whether the pattern interrupt + sensory hijack landed.
  • Hold rate (15s view / impression) — telegraphs whether the structure earned the watch.
  • Click-through rate — telegraphs whether the CTA earned the click.
  • CAC — the only metric that decides if the spot stays in rotation. Below target = winner. Above target = library de-weighted.
The compounding effect

Every winning spot reinforces its pattern card's weight in the library. After 30 spots, the Recommender's selections are noticeably sharper. After 100, the system has its own defensible point of view about what works for which brand category. This is the moat.

10 · Viral Variables

What actually makes an AI ad go viral.

The Factory optimizes against 24 measurable variables across six layers. Not all matter equally. The four marked ★ HIGH LEVERAGE below are the variables that, in our deconstruction of 30+ winning cinematic ads, account for the majority of variance in hook rate, hold rate, and CAC. Everything else amplifies these or kills them in QA.

A · Visual & Cinematic Execution

V01 · ★ HIGH LEVERAGE

Pattern interrupt.

The first 0.5s before the hook lands. A single unexpected frame, object, or composition that makes the thumb stop. Most AI ads die here — they look like AI ads at frame zero, and the audience scrolls past before the hook even gets a chance.

Owner · Stage Designer #1 · Beat 01
V02 · ★ HIGH LEVERAGE

Sensory hijack.

The ASMR pour, the macro skin texture, the slo-mo fizz, the bite. Bypasses the rational brain — creates a felt experience through the screen. This is what separates "an ad" from "a moment." First six seconds.

Owner · Stage Designer #2 · Beat 02
V03

Visual identity lock.

The thing that makes someone screenshot a frame. Color palette, lens choice, lighting signature. Without this, AI ads collapse into "AI ad" sameness. Every frame should look like it could only be from this brand.

Owner · Skills Curator + all Stage Designers
V04

Pacing & cut rhythm.

Different from edits. Edits are the cuts; pacing is the velocity of cuts mapped to emotional beats. Most AI ads fail because they're either too cut-heavy (TikTok brain) or too slow (broadcast brain). Genre dictates rhythm.

Owner · Deploy agent · genre-specific cut profile
V05

Camera grammar.

Handheld vs. dolly vs. crane vs. whip-pan vs. static. Each carries genre meaning. AI ads that pick the wrong camera language read as "AI" instantly — a luxury minimal spot can't have whip pans, an athletic hero spot can't be locked off.

Owner · Shape agent · per-beat lens grammar
V06

Color coherence.

The single biggest tell of unprofessional AI ads. Brand palette adherence across all 8 beats. Every frame must read as the same brand — no accidental color drift between Stage Designers.

Owner · Eval agent · 8/10 threshold

B · Narrative & Structure

V07 · ★ HIGH LEVERAGE

Narrative structure fit.

Scripting is the words; structure is the shape. Hook–Tension–Reveal vs. Transformation vs. Character–Conflict. Most failed AI ads have decent lines on the wrong skeleton. The Recommender's #1 job is matching structure to audience awareness.

Owner · Recommender agent · Gate 01
V08

Tension & stakes.

The "why care" beat. Almost every viral ad has a moment where it's clear this matters before the product enters. Without stakes, the spot is a brochure. Even a 15s spot needs 2-3s of stakes.

Owner · Shape agent · Beat 02-03
V09

Transformation moment.

The visible before/after. Even non-DTC ads have one — a state change, a felt shift, a world that's different. Without it, claims feel hollow. Always shown, never told.

Owner · Stage Designer #4 · Beat 04
V10

Payoff /punchline.

The moment of resolution. Comedic, emotional, or visual. The shareability driver — the thing the audience wants to send to a friend. Without a payoff, hold rate craters in the back half.

Owner · Shape agent · Beats 05-06

C · Performance & DR Mechanics

V11

CTA architecture.

Not just the words. Where it sits, how long it holds, verbal vs. text vs. both, how it ties to the offer. CTA gets ≥3 seconds and must be unambiguous. The cinematic experience earns the click here or not at all.

Owner · Shape + Deploy agents · Beat 06
V12

Offer clarity.

The actual deal — free shipping, BOGO, bundle, urgency. Often the difference between a viral ad with no sales and a winner. Unclear offer = unclear conversion path = wasted spend.

Owner · Intake agent · dr.offer field
V13

Hook ↔ hold tradeoff.

These compete. A pattern interrupt that doesn't earn the watch is a fail. A slow-burn hook with no stop power never gets the hold rate measured. Structure decision determines the trade.

Owner · Recommender + Eval agents
V14

Platform fit.

Same spot performs differently on TikTok, Reels, Feed, CTV. Aspect ratio, safe zones, sound-on assumptions, expected pacing all differ. v1.5 of the Factory will produce per-platform variants from one hero.

Owner · Deploy agent · v1.5 roadmap

D · Trust & Credibility

V15

Proof points.

Testimonial flashes, study citations, before/after evidence, founder appearance. AI ads especially need these because they trigger skepticism. At least one proof point must surface in the spot.

Owner · Shape agent · product.proof_points
V16

Authenticity signals.

Anti-polish moments. Grain, imperfect handheld, real audio, founder presence. The most effective AI ads include something that looks "real" to anchor trust — counter-intuitive but consistent across winners.

Owner · Stage Designers + Deploy
V17 · ★ HIGH LEVERAGE

Character / face.

Even product-led spots need a face for emotional anchoring. Eye contact in the first 5 seconds is one of the highest-correlation features with hold rate. If there's no face, brand recall craters.

Owner · Shape + Stage Designer #3
V18

Risk calibration.

Three types — claim risk (regulatory), taste risk (audience offense), aesthetic risk (style alienation). Bold attempts need explicit risk classification, not just "let's be edgy." Each type has a different mitigation path.

Owner · Eval agent · regulatory check

E · Brand & Memory

V19 · ★ HIGH LEVERAGE

Memorability anchor.

The single iconic frame the viewer could draw 24 hours later. If there isn't one, brand recall craters. The hero shot is the candidate; the question is whether it's iconic enough to survive 24 hours of feed.

Owner · Stage Designer #5 · Beat 05
V20

Brand world.

The recurring visual signature across spots — Liquid Death's gothic absurdism, Apple's white void, Magic Spoon's pastel surreal. Makes ad #5 reinforce ads #1-4. Compounds across a campaign.

Owner · Skills Curator · brand asset pack
V21

Sonic branding.

Beyond sound effects: a recurring musical motif, jingle, or audio signature. Sonic identity compounds across a campaign and creates instant recognition mid-scroll, even with sound off and just rhythm visible.

Owner · Deploy + Skills Curator
V22

Cultural timing.

Trend-rides have ceiling but expire fast. Evergreen compounds. Most spots should be evergreen-first with optional trend layer. Trend-only spots are a separate workflow with shorter shelf life.

Owner · Recommender + Improve agents

F · Production Discipline

V23

AI artifact QA.

The thing that kills AI ads. Finger weirdness, morphing logos, inconsistent product across cuts, eye drift, hallucinated text. Needs explicit checks at every Stage Designer output and final QA at Eval.

Owner · Stage Designers + Eval agent
V24

Product fidelity.

The SKU has to look exactly like the SKU. Most AI tools drift on packaging, label text, color, proportions. Hero shot needs explicit verification or hybrid live action — this is the most common reason a spot fails Gate 3.

Owner · Eval agent · pixel-level check vs. brand asset pack

The five highest leverage variables.

From the 24 variables, five drive the majority of hook rate, hold rate, and CAC variance. These get prioritized in every Eval pass and weighted highest in the library scoring:

★ The five

1. Pattern interrupt in the first 0.5s · 2. Sensory hijack within 6 seconds · 3. Narrative structure matched to audience awareness · 4. Character / face with eye contact in first 5s · 5. Memorability anchor — the single iconic frame.

Everything else either amplifies these or kills them in QA. If a brief is constrained on time or budget, ship the five. The other 19 are differentiators, not foundations.

11 · Standard Operating Procedure

Brief to shipped spot.

The end-to-end procedure the Ad Pros team runs for every Cinematic Factory engagement. Default turnaround is Tier 2 (Standard, 72 hours). Each step lists the owner, action, output, and time budget. Three human gates are non-negotiable — everything else is automated through the agent pipeline.

Phase 0 · Pre-engagement

Before any brief enters the Factory, the brand must clear three qualification gates:

  • Tier selection — Spark, Standard, Premium, or Bespoke. Sales/account team confirms tier with the brand based on stakes, budget, and timeline.
  • Product category eligibility — does the brand fall in the Factory's six supported categories (supplement, beverage, skincare, wearable, apparel, food, health-tech)? Edge cases route to a Bespoke conversation.
  • Hybrid live action check — does the brief trigger one of the five hybrid requirements (real product physics, founder presence, talent licensing, regulatory environment, brand maturity)? If yes, route to Tier 4 immediately.

SOP discipline

The SOP is non-negotiable. The three gates are non-negotiable. The retry caps are non-negotiable. The whole point of the Factory is reproducibility — and reproducibility comes from the procedure, not the prompts. If the procedure breaks, the agents start producing inconsistent output, and the library learning loop poisons itself. Run the SOP. Every time. Without exception.

12 · Roadmap

From v1.0 to v2.0.

v1.0 ships the 8-agent pipeline against the 30-spot seed library, four intake tiers, and three narrative structures. v1.x adds modular cutdowns and platform-specific re-edits. v2.0 introduces autonomous library expansion and predictive performance modeling.

Phase 01 · v1.0 — Ship the spine

  • Library seed: deconstruct 30 reference cinematic ads into pattern cards (5 per genre).
  • Schema lock: finalize the 5-layer variable schema in BigQuery + Supabase.
  • Agent build-out: ship Recommender, Intake, Shape, 8 Stage Designers, Skills Curator, Eval, Deploy, Improve.
  • Three gates wired into ClickUp: Brand approval (G1), Creative approval (G2), Final approval (G3).
  • First five live spots across at least 3 product categories — supplement, beverage, health-tech.

Phase 02 · v1.5 — Cutdowns & platforms

  • Modular re-edit pipeline: auto-generate 6s, 10s, 15s cutdowns from the 30s hero against per-platform safe zones.
  • Aspect ratio variants: 9:16, 1:1, 4:5, 16:9 with per-aspect framing rules.
  • Per-platform Eval: separate scoring rubrics for TikTok, Meta Reels, Meta Feed, YouTube Shorts, CTV.
  • A/B variant generator: hook-only, CTA-only, and music-only variants from a single hero.

Phase 03 · v2.0 — The flywheel

  • Autonomous library expansion: the system scrapes Meta Ad Library + TikTok Top Ads daily, deconstructs new winners, adds to pattern cards without human intervention.
  • Predictive CAC modeling: Eval predicts CAC range pre-launch based on pattern card performance history. Brands see expected DR floor before approving.
  • Self-tuning prompts: Skills Curator runs nightly regression — winning prompts get pinned, losing prompts get retired.
  • Cross-brand pattern transfer: a winning beat sequence from a beverage brand becomes a recommendable structure for a supplement brand.

Open questions.

These are the architectural decisions that need a Nehal call before v1.0 build:

  • Library priority: which 5 brands per genre form the seed? (proposed list lives in ClickUp doc cinematic_factory.seed_library)
  • Eval thresholds per tier: are 7/7 (Standard) and 8/8 (Premium) the right gates, or should Premium be 8.5/8?
  • Gate 3 fail policy: if a brand rejects at G3, do we eat the re-render cost or charge against retainer?
  • External rollout: does this become an Ad Pros service tier, or is it productized as a standalone SaaS?
  • Pricing: Tier 1 → Tier 4 — at what spread does this make economic sense given compute + retainer math?
Recommended next step

Schedule a 90-minute build session to lock the seed library (which 30 reference ads), the Eval thresholds per tier, and the pricing spread. Once those three decisions are made, v1.0 build is a straight line through the Workflow Factory architecture.
